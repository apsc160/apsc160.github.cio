Description of the Problem

In the in-lab component, part 2, you generated two data files (two traces) using the hardware module. In this part of the lab, you will use Excel to generate plots of this data. You should create one Excel file containing two Excel worksheets, one for each of the data files that you generated.

1. Calculating X & Y Coordinates

To plot your data, you will need to determine the X & Y coordinates of the end-point of the arm. In your data files from In-Lab Part 2, you have the voltage of the base and elbow sensors. The following are the basic steps to convert those voltage values into X & Y coordinates:
  1. Using your results from part 1, convert the voltage values into angles. Specifically, use the slope and intercept of the angle vs. voltage plots. This calculation will give you the relative angles for both the base sensor and the elbow sensor.
  2. Convert the relative angles to absolute angles. For the base sensor, the relative and absolute angles are the same, but for the elbow sensor they are different.
  3. Using the absolute angles, and the lengths of the base and elbow segments, determine the values of the X & Y coordinates. This is the same calculation that was performed in Lab 1. Recall that you measured and recorded the lengths of the segments in the In-Lab Part 1.

Recall that for the arm layout above, with angles measured in radians in a counter-clockwise direction. We can relate the three angles together using parallel-line angle rules, and the position of the arm's end-point can be computed using basic trigonometry:

$$\begin{aligned} \theta_2 & = \theta_1 + \theta_3 - \pi\\ x & = AB\cos(\theta_1) + BC\cos(\theta_2)\\ y & = AB\sin(\theta_1) + BC\sin(\theta_2) \end{aligned}$$

You can put all of the above calculations into a single equation in excel, or split them across several equations. In either case, your Excel file should contain all of the equations that you used. Do not just put the final values into your Excel file. The TAs must see the expressions used to generate your results.

2. Generating Plots

For each of your data files, you should generate a plot of the X & Y coordinates of the data (with the points joined by lines). If all your calculations were done correctly, then the plot should give you a good approximation of the original figure. There may be some rotation or distortion of the figure. A certain amount is likely, but you should be able to easily recognize the original figures.
